Replit's pushback is right on both counts, and the practical build priority at the end is the sharpest thing in the whole exchange. Let me build on it.
The rep default problem is real and solvable in the same pattern. Rather than defaulting to "everyone is a rep" or asking upfront "who are your reps," the inference engine should do what any RevOps analyst would do in the first 10 minutes: look at deal ownership distribution. If 6 people own 95% of deals and 8 others own 1-2 each, that's a detectable pattern. The instant audit output should say "We identified 6 active sellers based on deal ownership patterns" and list them — not as a config screen, but as a finding. "These 8 other owners (support@, admin@, etc.) own 14 deals totaling $420K. We excluded them from rep metrics. Adjust →." That's the same inline correction pattern, but applied before the user sees pipeline numbers, not after. The inference runs during the sync wait, the exclusion happens before skill execution, and the correction affordance ships with the first output.
The confidence-as-methodology framing is the design challenge. Replit nailed this. The difference between "we're not sure" and "here's how we got here" is entirely a design problem, not a data problem. Think about how financial analysts present assumptions — they don't hedge, they state: "Assumes 6-month lookback, new business pipeline only, 14-day stale threshold." That reads as rigor, not uncertainty. Pandora's assumption disclosure should feel like a methodology sidebar on every finding card, not a warning banner. Something like a small "How we calculated this" expandable that shows the three assumptions that matter most for that specific finding, each with a one-click override.
Where this changes your build sequence:
The highest-leverage work right now isn't a new onboarding flow — it's exactly what Replit said. You need a FindingCard component pattern that every skill output renders through, and that component has three things baked in: the insight itself, the assumptions that produced it (pulled from ConfigAssumptions), and correction affordances that write back to workspace config when clicked. That's one component that restructures the entire configuration UX.
The build order I'd suggest: first, define the FindingCard schema — what does a skill output need to include for inline assumptions and corrections to work? Second, retrofit the instant audit skills (pipeline-hygiene, data-quality, single-thread, coverage) to emit findings in that schema. Third, build the correction-to-config writeback so clicking "include these deals" actually patches the workspace config. Fourth, make the onboarding flow nothing more than: connect CRM → sync → instant audit → findings delivered.
