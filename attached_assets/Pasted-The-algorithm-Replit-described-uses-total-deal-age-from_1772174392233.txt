The algorithm Replit described uses total deal age from created_at against historical win patterns — median of ~12 days, p75 of ~93 days. So the thresholds shake out to:

Fast: ≤12 days
On Track: 12–93 days
Slowing: 93–186 days
Stalled: 186+ days

Now look at the chart. Pilot deals are obviously going to be old — they've already gone through Discovery, Demo Scheduled, and Demo Done before they even get to Pilot. Of course they're 186+ days from creation. For Frontera's sales motion selling into healthcare organizations, a 6-month cycle for a pilot deal is probably completely normal. But the algorithm is painting it all red.
And that's the kill shot. A VP of RevOps opens this page, sees a wall of red, and one of two things happens: they either panic and start escalating deals that are actually progressing fine, or — more likely — they stop trusting the tool within a week. "Everything is stalled" is the same as "nothing is stalled." You've destroyed the signal.
The root problem is the benchmark is monolithic — one set of thresholds applied uniformly across stages. But deal velocity isn't uniform. A deal should spend 3 days in Demo Scheduled and should spend 60 days in Pilot. Those are fundamentally different expectations. Measuring both against the same created_at clock is like grading a freshman and a senior on the same "years since enrollment" metric and concluding the senior is behind.
Here's what I'd fix:
Stage-specific velocity benchmarks. Instead of one global clock, each stage gets its own expected duration derived from historical wins. "How long do winning deals typically spend in Pilot?" That's the number you benchmark against — not total deal age. When Apricott shows 63 days in Pilot, the question isn't "is 63 days old for a deal?" but "is 63 days long for Pilot specifically?" Those are completely different questions with completely different answers.
Segment the benchmarks by deal characteristics. A $3k deal and a $300k deal don't move at the same speed. An enterprise healthcare prospect and an SMB don't either. The thresholds should flex based on deal size, pipeline, maybe account segment. This is where the workspace config inference work you've already done could really pay off — auto-detecting these segments from CRM patterns rather than requiring manual setup.
Relative ranking instead of (or alongside) absolute thresholds. Rather than hard cutoffs that can make everything red, show where each deal sits relative to its peers. "This Pilot deal is in the 90th percentile for time-in-stage" is more useful than "stalled" because it inherently adjusts to whatever the actual distribution looks like. Even if your sales cycles are long, you can still identify the outliers.
Composite health scoring. This ties back to the earlier point about the red-list-green-detail dissonance. Combine the velocity signal with engagement signals (recent calls, multi-threading changes, stakeholder coverage). A deal that's been in Pilot for 90 days with active calls and expanding contacts is in a fundamentally different bucket than one that's been in Pilot for 90 days with no activity for 3 weeks. One gets a "long but healthy" tag, the other gets "at risk — going dark."
The goal is to make the red dots mean something. When a VP sees 3 deals flagged red out of 20 instead of 15 out of 20, they pay attention. That's a triageable number. That's actionable. A wall of red is just noise.
This feels like it could be a meaningful upgrade to the Coaching Intelligence skill — want to think through the stage-specific benchmarking spec?
